# Compliance Tips Library

Short, educational facts and tips about residential compliance in California. Use these as the basis for social media posts, email content, and website copy.

Each tip should be factually accurate. Do not publish tips without verifying the underlying compliance requirement.

---

## Smoke Detectors

**Tip:** California requires smoke detectors in every bedroom, in the hallway outside sleeping areas, and on every level of the home — including the basement. Missing even one can flag during escrow.

**Tip:** As of 2014, California requires that smoke detectors in existing single-family homes be replaced with 10-year sealed-battery models or hardwired units when batteries need replacement. Many older homes still have non-compliant detectors.

**Tip:** Smoke detectors have an expiration date — usually 10 years from manufacture. An expired detector is a compliance issue even if the battery still works.

---

## Carbon Monoxide Detectors

**Tip:** California law requires CO detectors in all dwellings with an attached garage, a gas appliance, or a fireplace. This covers the vast majority of homes in LA and OC.

**Tip:** CO detectors must be placed outside each sleeping area. A single detector in the living room does not meet requirements for a multi-bedroom home.

---

## Water Heater Strapping

**Tip:** California requires water heaters to be secured with two straps — one in the upper third and one in the lower third of the unit. A single strap does not meet code.

**Tip:** An unstrapped water heater can tip over in an earthquake and rupture a gas line, creating a serious fire and explosion risk. Strapping is one of the most important seismic safety measures a homeowner can take.

**Tip:** Water heater strapping is one of the most commonly flagged compliance issues in LA County real estate transactions.

---

## Overflow Pipes

**Tip:** Your water heater's temperature-pressure relief (TPR) valve requires a discharge pipe routed to a safe location — not just left dangling. An improperly terminated pipe is a compliance failure and a safety hazard.

---

## Low-Flow Fixtures

**Tip:** California requires toilets manufactured after 1994 to use no more than 1.6 gallons per flush (gpf). Many older homes still have 3.5 gpf or higher toilets. Replacing them before listing is required.

**Tip:** Showerheads must flow at no more than 2.5 gallons per minute (gpm) to meet California's low-flow requirements. Pre-2000 showerheads often exceed this limit.

---

## Seismic Gas Shutoff Valves

**Tip:** A seismic gas shutoff valve automatically closes the gas supply when it detects earthquake activity above a set threshold. This can prevent post-earthquake fires caused by gas leaks.

**Tip:** Not all California jurisdictions require seismic gas shutoff valves, but some cities in LA County do. A compliance inspection will determine whether your property requires one.

---

## General

**Tip:** Compliance inspections are not the same as general home inspections. A compliance inspection focuses on specific legally mandated safety and conservation items. A general home inspector looks at the overall condition of the property.

**Tip:** Compliance issues discovered late in escrow are stressful and expensive. Getting a compliance inspection early — before listing — gives sellers time to address issues without pressure.

**Tip:** Real estate agents are not compliance experts. Many agents refer clients to a dedicated compliance service to ensure accuracy and protect their professional reputation.
