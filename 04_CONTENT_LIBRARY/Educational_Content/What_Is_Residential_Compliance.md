# What Is Residential Compliance?

*A plain-language explainer for homeowners and sellers. Can be adapted for blog, social media, or email.*

---

If you've been told your home needs to be "in compliance" before it can be sold, you're not alone. For many homeowners, compliance is an unfamiliar term that shows up at exactly the worst moment — when they're already deep into a stressful transaction.

Here's what it means, and why it matters.

---

## Compliance Is a Legal Requirement

California law requires that residential properties meet certain safety and conservation standards before they can be sold. These requirements exist to protect buyers, occupants, and communities.

They cover things like:

- **Fire safety** — Are there working smoke detectors in the right locations?
- **Gas safety** — Is the water heater properly secured so it won't tip in an earthquake?
- **Carbon monoxide protection** — Are CO detectors installed where required?
- **Water conservation** — Do the toilets, showerheads, and faucets meet California's water efficiency standards?
- **Seismic safety** — Is there an automatic gas shutoff valve if one is required by local code?

---

## Why Does This Come Up During a Sale?

California law requires sellers to disclose whether their property meets these requirements. If items are out of compliance, they typically need to be corrected before close of escrow.

Buyers and their agents expect compliance. Lenders may also require it. Sellers who miss compliance items can face delays, renegotiation, or deals that fall through entirely.

---

## What a Compliance Inspection Covers

A compliance inspection evaluates all the required items for your property:

- Smoke detectors (presence, placement, type)
- Carbon monoxide detectors
- Water heater strapping
- Water heater overflow pipe
- Low-flow plumbing fixtures
- Seismic gas shutoff valve (if applicable)

After the inspection, you'll know exactly what — if anything — needs to be corrected.
